# Conclusion — Being Amazonian Beyond Amazon

### Executive Summary

The book closes by making the transferability claim explicit: the authors argue that the mechanisms documented in Parts One and Two — Leadership Principles, Bar Raiser hiring, single-threaded teams, written narratives, Working Backwards, input-metric discipline, long-term equity compensation — are not artifacts specific to Amazon's scale or industry, but a general operating system that other companies, nonprofits, and community organizations can adopt. The chapter is candid that adoption isn't guaranteed to succeed (it depends on genuine executive-level buy-in, not just enthusiasm from someone who used to work at Amazon), but argues the more common outcome, when tried seriously, is success — and closes with a concrete, prioritized starting checklist rather than a purely conceptual argument.

---

### 1. What Adoption Actually Costs

The authors are explicit that "being Amazonian" is not a free upgrade — it requires giving up things that feel comfortable:

- **Autonomy is intense, not liberating in the way it sounds.** Separable, single-threaded teams require real organizational redesign — someone has to give up authority, jurisdiction, or "turf" for the structure to work.
- **Narrative writing exposes you.** Presenting an idea as a six-pager means accepting open critique of your own reasoning from anyone in the room, with no charisma or slide polish to fall back on.
- **Input-metric discipline is culturally unfamiliar.** Most people are evaluated (and evaluate others) on outputs; learning to manage the controllable inputs instead takes patient trial and error, not a one-time switch.
- **Long-term equity compensation cuts against the norm.** Most Western companies tie pay to short-term goal achievement; committing leaders to multi-year "return on work" via equity is a real departure, not a talking point.

### 2. What Adoption Returns

Set against those costs, the chapter frames the payoff in both organizational and personal terms:

- **For the company:** sustained risk-taking, openness to ideas from any level, and — critically — a different relationship with failure. A failed initiative that was executed in good faith and adherent to the principles is treated as a **systemic learning event**, not an individual's mark of shame, because many people (interviewers, reviewers, approvers) shared ownership of the decision along the way.
- **For the individual:** the chapter argues the reward isn't just career outcomes but the satisfaction of tackling hard, well-scoped problems under real constraints and seeing them through — the same "being Amazonian" identity the authors say never fully leaves you after you've internalized it.

### 3. The Starting Checklist

The authors close with eight concrete starting points, explicitly ordered from fastest/lowest-friction to more structurally demanding:

1. **Ban PowerPoint** for complex topics; require six-page narratives and PR/FAQs instead. Fast to implement, meets initial resistance, but reliably produces a "we can never go back" reaction once leaders experience it.
2. **Establish Bar Raiser hiring.** No longer Amazon-exclusive; delivers near-term improvement in hire quality and a training/learning side-effect for everyone in the interview loop.
3. **Focus on controllable input metrics** — without abandoning output metrics. This is explicitly the slowest of the eight to get right, since finding metrics that truly predict your outputs requires sustained trial and error (see chapter 6's Fast Track In Stock evolution).
4. **Reorganize around autonomous, single-threaded teams**, starting in product development before expanding elsewhere, and expect friction over authority and dependencies along the way.
5. **Revise compensation structures** toward long-term incentives, applied consistently rather than carved out for "special cases."
6. **Articulate your culture explicitly** — long-term thinking, customer obsession, invention, operational excellence, or your own equivalents — and build it into actual processes and discussions, not posters.
7. **Define leadership principles collaboratively**, written by the people who will live by them (not outsourced to a consultant), revisited and revised over time, and embedded into hiring and product development from day one.
8. **Depict your own flywheel** — the specific, causally connected drivers of growth in your business — and evaluate every initiative by whether it strengthens or weakens that loop.

---

### Bottom Line

The authors' closing caveat matters as much as the checklist: Amazon's specific approach isn't presented as the only valid way to run a company, and plenty of successful companies operate differently. The claim is narrower and more useful — that few companies have matched Amazon's combined record of sustained growth, invention, and expansion into genuinely new businesses, which makes the underlying mechanisms worth evaluating on their own merits rather than dismissing as culturally specific to Amazon.

*Source: Conclusion, "Being Amazonian Beyond Amazon," in Colin Bryar & Bill Carr, Working Backwards (St. Martin's Publishing Group, 2021).*
