# Appendix A — Interview Feedback Examples

Companion material to [Chapter 2 (Hiring: The Bar Raiser Process)](../Part%20One/Being%20Amazonian/02-hiring.md). The appendix pairs a weak and a strong interview write-up for the same fictional candidate ("Joe") to make concrete what chapter 2 argues in the abstract: written feedback is only useful if it is specific and verbatim, not a summary impression.

### What Makes Feedback Weak

The weak example is fluent and confident-sounding, but on inspection contains no usable data: it describes the candidate's *background* ("solid background owning and driving strategy") rather than *specific things he personally did*, includes no actual interview questions or answers, and offers no way for a hiring committee to independently judge whether the praise is justified. A reader has to simply trust the interviewer's impression.

### What Makes Feedback Strong

The strong example includes the interviewer's verdict up front (not inclined to hire) *and* the verbatim Q&A that produced it — allowing any reader to reach their own conclusion independent of the interviewer's framing. The transcript demonstrates the STAR-style probing described in chapter 2 in action: the interviewer keeps drilling past a plausible-sounding answer ("Biz dev deal... the strategic output was really big for us") until the candidate is forced to reveal his actual role ("I was one of three members of the deal team... my role was relationship manager") — surfacing the gap between team accomplishment and personal contribution that chapter 2 identifies as the single most common thing weak feedback fails to separate. A second exchange in the same transcript shows the candidate waffling and failing to tie a product suggestion back to a strategic rationale when pressed — recorded verbatim rather than summarized as "weak strategic thinking," so the reader sees the failure directly instead of taking the interviewer's word for it.

### The Transferable Rule

Good interview feedback reads like a transcript with commentary, not a character reference. If a reader who wasn't in the room can't tell *why* the interviewer reached their conclusion from the write-up alone, the feedback isn't finished.

*Source: Appendix A, in Colin Bryar & Bill Carr, Working Backwards (St. Martin's Publishing Group, 2021).*
