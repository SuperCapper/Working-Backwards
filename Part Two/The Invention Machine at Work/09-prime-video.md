# Chapter 9 — Prime Video

### Executive Summary

Where the Kindle chapter shows Amazon's mechanisms working cleanly toward a validated bet, Prime Video is the book's account of what happens when a company **doesn't control either end of the value chain** — and takes over six years, a public launch-day failure, and a string of externally-driven disruptions (Netflix, Hulu, platform politics) to fight its way out of the middle. The throughline: Amazon's first digital video attempt (Amazon Unbox, 2006) failed specifically because the team prioritized *competitor* obsession over *customer* obsession, and the eventual fix wasn't a single clever idea but a repositioning strategy — deliberately move to the content-creation end (Amazon Studios) and the device-consumption end (Fire Tablet, Fire TV) of the value chain, the same structural logic chapter 7 describes for Kindle.

---

### 1. Unbox: A Failure Amazon Owned Publicly

Amazon's first digital video service launched in September 2006 to a live on-stage demo where the video played upside down — a fitting preview of the launch itself. Root causes, as Bill Carr later documented in his own brutally self-critical performance review (explicitly invoking the Earn Trust principle's "vocally self-critical" standard, grading his own performance a "D"):

- **Competitor obsession over customer obsession.** Rushed to market to avoid losing a rumored race with Apple, the team shipped past unresolved problems instead of using beta feedback to slow down and fix them.
- **Technical constraints compounded by a quality-over-speed trade-off.** Downloads (not streaming — not yet technically viable) took one to four hours for a two-hour movie; the team optimized for DVD-quality picture, which only made downloads slower and more failure-prone, while YouTube (launched months earlier) proved customers tolerated low resolution in exchange for speed and ease.
- **Platform lock-out.** Unbox ran only on Windows PCs under three years old, relying on buggy Microsoft DRM, because Apple's proprietary FairPlay DRM (paired with exclusive studio deals) was never going to be licensed to a competitor.
- **Studio-imposed rights constraints Amazon had no leverage to change.** A decades-old "blackout window" clause gave pay-TV channels (HBO, Showtime, Starz) exclusive rights to a film for roughly three years after a brief 60–90 day digital sales window — a constraint the studios acknowledged was outdated but wouldn't renegotiate, since short-term financial incentives (unlike Amazon's own long-term-equity compensation, chapter 1) drove their decision-making. The clause wasn't removed until 2013.

Bezos's response to the failure is presented as a case study in the long-term people investment described in chapter 1: rather than firing Bill Carr, he reportedly framed it as "I just made a million-dollar investment in you... figure out where you went wrong, document it, share it, and don't make the same mistake again." He separately invoked the "Howard Hughes model" (from the film *The Aviator* — Hughes running his fingers along an aircraft fuselage demanding it be flush and clean) to instruct Steve Kessel that insisting on uncompromising customer-experience standards was now explicitly his job.

### 2. A Partial Fix, Then Two External Disruptions

Amazon's frugal, low-budget product offsite (a walk to another Seattle office building, no travel) produced one workable idea from a new business-development hire, Josh Kramer: partnering with TiVo to give Unbox a "path to the living room" via progressive download to a set-top box — a genuine, if incremental, improvement. But the win was almost immediately overshadowed by two moves Amazon initially underestimated:

- **Netflix's "Watch Now" (January 2007)** combined subscription and streaming, bundling a modest catalog as an *included* benefit of the existing DVD-by-mail subscription — solving the cold-start problem of getting people to pay for a new, unproven service. Amazon (like Time Warner's chairman, who compared the threat to "the Albanian army") dismissed it as a half-baked test.
- **Hulu (October 2007)**, backed by studio owners News Corp and NBC Universal, offered next-day network TV episodes for free with ads — directly undercutting Unbox's $2.99-per-episode model and killing sales of Amazon's top-selling shows overnight, with no negotiating leverage available since the studios themselves owned the competitor.

### 3. Connected TVs and the Limits of Being a Pure Distributor

Rebranded Amazon Video On Demand (2008) and expanded to smart TVs and Roku, but plagued by uneven "rebuffering" quality across devices and providers — addressed with an automated proactive-refund system (three-or-more rebuffers triggers an automatic refund), which Bezos highlighted approvingly in a shareholder letter as a model of customer-first automation. Meanwhile, Amazon was **excluded from Xbox and PlayStation** for years — the console makers ran competing à la carte video stores and didn't view subscription-only Netflix as a threat, while retail rivals Walmart and Best Buy pressured electronics manufacturers not to carry Amazon's app. By the chapter's account, roughly 95% of Netflix's streams at the time came through channels (consoles, iOS) that were closed to Amazon.

### 4. Naming the Real Problem: Stuck in the Value-Chain Valley

Applying the same value-chain framework introduced in the Kindle chapter, the team concluded Amazon Video On Demand was trapped in the unprofitable **middle**: no control over content (unlike HBO or the studios), no control over playback devices (unlike Apple or the console makers) — meaning none of Amazon's usual retail-flywheel advantages (price, selection, cost structure) could differentiate it in digital video at all, since any competitor could match the same catalog on the same terms. The strategic response, beginning in 2010, was to deliberately move to both ends simultaneously: **content** (Amazon Studios) and **devices** (Fire Tablet, Fire TV).

### 5. Prime Video: The "Oh-by-the-Way" Insight

Facing Netflix's content-licensing budget (already double initial estimates), Amazon's team spent weeks failing to find a differentiated subscription concept or viable hardware idea (a universal remote, an early ancestor of the Echo "puck," a preloaded set-top box) before Bezos proposed something no one had listed: **make video streaming a free, included Prime benefit rather than a standalone paid product** — explicitly modeled on how Netflix itself had bootstrapped streaming as an add-on to its existing DVD subscriber base rather than launching it as a cold-start product. The underlying economic insight: subscription video is a **fixed-cost business** — licensing fees don't scale with viewership, so once a subscriber base is large enough, incremental subscription revenue is close to pure profit. Bundling video into Prime's already-large membership base solved the classic subscription chicken-and-egg problem (need subscribers to afford content; need content to attract subscribers) by outsourcing subscriber acquisition to Prime's existing fast-shipping value proposition, buying time for the catalog to improve. It also created a strategic moat: a competitor could clone free shipping, or could build a Netflix-style service, but replicating *both* combined was much harder.

### 6. The LOVEFiLM Lesson: Licensed Content Isn't a Moat

Acquiring European DVD/streaming service LOVEFiLM was meant to give Amazon a head start over Netflix in the UK and Germany — until Netflix entered the same content-licensing bids and simply outbid Amazon for the same titles, driving studio fees up for both. The takeaway that reshaped strategy: **licensed content can always be out-bid**, so escaping the value-chain trap required actually **owning content**, not just paying more for someone else's.

### 7. Devices: Building the Other End of the Chain

Because Amazon had already built internal hardware capability for Kindle, it could pivot the same organization toward video-capable devices: **Fire Tablet** (Nov 2011, $199, undercutting the iPad, quickly the #2 tablet by unit sales) and **Fire TV** (April 2014, $99, with voice search designed to close what the team called the "10-foot UI" gap between couch and screen). Internally, device projects were tracked by anonymized letters (Fire TV was "Project B / Bueller") specifically to limit the blast radius of any information leak.

### 8. Amazon Studios: Becoming a Content Creator on Amazon's Own Terms

Amazon Studios launched by applying its own culture unmodified to Hollywood norms it didn't share — Bar Raiser interviews for development executives, a $160K salary cap, no cash bonuses, coach travel — creating friction with an industry used to different incentives. Its most distinctively Amazonian wrinkle: rather than trusting executive taste alone, Amazon **released pilot episodes publicly for viewer feedback and ratings data before deciding which to greenlight to full series** — a customer-data-driven greenlighting process the chapter frames as more Amazonian (and more rigorous) than the traditional studio system. Netflix's *House of Cards* (Feb 2013) is credited with proving A-list talent could headline streaming-native productions, opening the door for Amazon's own critically recognized original series (*Transparent*, *The Man in the High Castle*, *Mozart in the Jungle*).

---

### Bottom Line

Prime Video's arc argues that a value-chain position, not effort or execution quality alone, sets the ceiling on what a business can achieve — Amazon's team could out-work its Unbox mistakes, but no amount of operational excellence in pure distribution could substitute for owning either content or the consumption device. The eventual fix required simultaneously conceding the middle of the chain and investing years, in parallel, at both ends of it — while still applying the same long-term-thinking, customer-obsessed principles (proactive refunds, data-driven greenlighting, "oh-by-the-way" bundling) that had worked everywhere else in the company.

*Source: Chapter 9, "Prime Video," in Colin Bryar & Bill Carr, Working Backwards (St. Martin's Publishing Group, 2021).*
