# Chapter 7 — Kindle

### Executive Summary

Kindle is the book's first full case study of Part One's mechanisms operating together under real pressure: single-threaded leadership, Working Backwards, the PR/FAQ, and a willingness to be "misunderstood for long periods of time" (and to badly overrun budget) in service of a long-term bet. In January 2004, Bezos concluded that Amazon's physical-media business (then 77% of company revenue) faced an existential digital transition it could not out-wait, and that meeting the threat required not a product decision but an **organizational** one: install a single-threaded leader over Digital, reporting directly to him, unencumbered by the existing retail business — the same structural move chapter 3 describes in the abstract, here shown at the scale of a bet-the-company initiative. The resulting product, Kindle, required Amazon to become something it had no core competency in — a hardware manufacturer — and succeeded specifically because of choices (build vs. outsource, invent vs. fast-follow, price near cost) that a conventional forward-looking business case would have rejected.

---

### 1. The Organizational Decision Came Before the Product Decision

Rather than immediately debating what digital product to build, Bezos's first move was to appoint Steve Kessel — then VP of the highly profitable physical media business — as SVP of a new Digital organization reporting directly to him, deliberately pulling Kessel out of retail entirely. The stated logic: embedding digital inside the existing (much larger, much more profitable) physical media org would guarantee digital always lost the fight for attention and resources. This is single-threaded leadership (chapter 3) applied at the highest possible stakes — the leader had to work on *nothing else* — and it only worked because Part One's other mechanisms (six-pagers and S-Team goals for the retail business Bezos was now spending less time on, PR/FAQs for the new Digital initiatives) let Bezos stay aligned with both halves of the company without being present for every decision in either.

### 2. Invent, Don't Fast-Follow — Even Under Public Pressure

Two scenes establish the strategic choice at the heart of the chapter. First, Steve Jobs personally demoed iTunes for Windows to Bezos and colleagues in 2003, predicting Amazon would become merely "the last place to buy CDs" — a taunt Bezos didn't visibly react to. Second, at a 2004 industry conference, a Universal Music executive publicly goaded Amazon from the podium for not yet being in digital music. In both cases, Bezos declined the obvious reactive move (rush out a copycat music service or press release). His explicit framework: a company can be a **fast follower** (viable, but strategically weak in a fast-moving market where a hasty copy will already be obsolete by the time it ships) or an **inventor** — and he wanted Amazon in the second category, even though invention has no roadmap and requires "the endurance to evaluate and discard many options."

### 3. Value-Chain Repositioning: Why Books, and Why Hardware

Amazon's physical-retail advantages — broadest selection, lowest cost structure — evaporated in digital: any competitor could aggregate the same catalog of files at similar hosting cost, so the middle of the value chain (Amazon's traditional position) offered no defensible edge. Bezos's reframing, sketched on a whiteboard for Kessel: move to **either end** of the value chain — content creation, or the devices/applications used to consume content (Apple's iPod/iTunes model). Amazon chose the consumption end, and chose **books** specifically over music or video, for concrete reasons: books were Amazon's largest and most identity-defining category; music already had an entrenched leader (Apple) with no compelling differentiated angle found in early PR/FAQ work; and video faced unresolved rights, bandwidth, and TV-connectivity barriers. E-books, by contrast, were a stagnant, neglected niche (readable only on a PC) with an obvious, unmet customer need.

### 4. The Build-vs-Outsource Decision

Committing to hardware meant committing to a capability — device manufacturing — Amazon had never had, provoking internal resistance (Bill Carr among the skeptics: *"We're an e-commerce company, not a hardware company!"*). The counter-argument, from Kessel and ultimately Bezos, generalizes well beyond Kindle:

- **You cannot outsource an integrated, differentiated customer experience.** An outsourced device vendor optimizes for many clients, not Amazon's specific customer-experience vision — the same logic the book applies elsewhere to Amazon's refusal to outsource e-commerce or fulfillment (Super Saver Shipping, Prime, FBA were only possible because Amazon owned the full stack).
- **Outsourcing compounds a short-term saving into a long-term structural disadvantage.** Every day Amazon iterated its own product, the gap with any outsourced competitor's static offering widened.
- **Owning the capability keeps the resulting IP and know-how in-house** rather than leaking it to a contract manufacturer who could resell similar capability to competitors.

Execution followed the same "who before what" logic as the org decision: Amazon hired hardware veteran Gregg Zehr (ex-Palm, ex-Apple) to build a dedicated hardware group (**Lab126**) based in Silicon Valley — accepting the novelty and risk of a remote center specifically because the talent didn't exist in Seattle — and acquired the small French startup **Mobipocket** to bootstrap e-reader software rather than build it from zero.

### 5. Design Principle: "Get Out of the Way"

Every major Kindle design decision was tested against a single customer-experience goal: the device should disappear so the reader connects directly with the content, the way a physical book does. Two technically risky, industry-unprecedented bets followed directly from that principle:

- **Whispernet** — free, built-in 3G wireless (via Sprint) eliminating "sideloading," the then-standard PC-tethered sync process that left most portable devices (the "stale iPod" problem) out of date. Amazon absorbed the carrier cost itself rather than pass it to customers, a controversial and technically difficult negotiation with wireless carriers who normally guarded direct customer relationships.
- **E Ink display** — an unproven MIT-spinoff technology, chosen despite giving up color and video and accepting slow page transitions, because it was readable in sunlight, easy on the eyes, and enabled roughly a week of battery life — trade-offs that only make sense if the design goal is disappearance, not feature maximalism.

Bezos personally reviewed physical prototypes (starting from Styrofoam mock-ups) at every stage, rejecting designs specifically when some element would "get in the way" of reading — not for aesthetic reasons.

### 6. Pricing and Selection: Investing Ahead of Profitability

Amazon launched with **90,000 e-books** (vs. Sony Reader's ~20,000), requiring publishers to digitize backlists that often no longer existed in digital form — leveraging know-how Amazon had already built through its Search Inside the Book feature. Pricing was set explicitly to grow a stagnant category rather than to maximize near-term margin: bestsellers and new releases at **$9.99**, close to Amazon's own wholesale cost, absorbing Whispernet's cost entirely, and pricing the device itself near cost as well — accepting a weak near-term P&L as a deliberate investment in the customer experience needed to make e-books a viable habit. When challenged in an OP1 review over Kindle's ballooning cost, Bezos's response — turning to the CFO and asking "How much money do we have?" — signaled the investment was a strategic commitment, not a runaway budget problem.

### 7. Launch and the Long Fuse

Kindle launched November 19, 2007 at $399, sold out in under six hours, and drew mixed reviews (critics noted the cheaper Sony Reader). Sustained demand followed restocking in February 2008, but the inflection point was Oprah Winfrey's October 2008 endorsement ("my new favorite favorite thing in the world"), which triggered a sales surge — though the book is careful to attribute Kindle's durability to the underlying product decisions, not the celebrity boost alone. Notably, by the time Kindle succeeded, Amazon's parallel digital music and video efforts were still small and struggling against Apple's head start — Kindle was a validated bet, not proof the whole Digital strategy had yet paid off.

---

### Bottom Line

Kindle demonstrates that Part One's mechanisms aren't independent tools — they compound. Single-threaded leadership freed Kessel's team to make an unpopular, non-consensus call (build hardware) without needing sign-off from people optimizing for the existing business; Working Backwards and the PR/FAQ process forced the team to justify that call against a specific customer experience rather than an internal capability gap; and the willingness to under-price and over-invest near-term reflected the same long-term-over-short-term logic embedded in Amazon's Ownership and Frugality principles (chapter 1) — frugality about wasted effort, not about strategic investment.

*Source: Chapter 7, "Kindle," in Colin Bryar & Bill Carr, Working Backwards (St. Martin's Publishing Group, 2021).*
