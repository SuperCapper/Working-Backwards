# Chapter 2 — Hiring: Amazon's Unique Bar Raiser Process

### Executive Summary

Most companies treat hiring as a series of individual judgment calls — a hiring manager's gut feel, filtered through whatever interview questions happen to occur to the room that day. Amazon's thesis is that this is a process failure, not a people failure: absent a structural safeguard, urgency bias, confirmation bias, and personal (similarity) bias will systematically degrade hiring quality as a company scales, silently rewriting its culture with every unvetted hire. The **Bar Raiser program**, created in 1999, is Amazon's mechanism for making a specific, non-negotiable standard — rather than the convenience of the moment — the deciding factor in every hire.

---

### 1. The Cost of Ad Hoc Hiring

The chapter opens with two illustrations of what happens without a rigorous process:

- **The unprepared-CEO anecdote:** A CEO interviewing a candidate for a COO role asked unfocused questions and lost the candidate — despite the decision's high stakes — because no one had thought about what information was actually needed to evaluate the person.

- **The "Green Corp." case:** A hiring manager under pressure to fill an open role let her team's enthusiasm substitute for rigor. The failure modes compound and are each named explicitly:
  - **Urgency bias** — an open headcount and an overworked team bias everyone toward a "yes."
  - **Confirmation bias** — interviewers debriefed with each other between rounds, so early positive impressions colored every later interview.
  - **Groupthink** — an unstructured decision meeting converged on consensus without genuine scrutiny.
  - **Lack of specificity** — written evaluations were generically positive ("solid background") with no verifiable, specific evidence of performance.
  - **Personal bias** — the unconscious tendency to favor candidates who resemble the interviewer (same school, background, life experience), which predicts nothing about job performance and narrows workforce diversity.

**Bottom line:** a bad hire is not a one-time cost. It drags down team standards for as long as they stay, and the team pays again — in wasted interview hours and re-opened searches — when the hire inevitably fails.

### 2. Origins: A Bottom-Up Fix, Not a Bezos Mandate

Contrary to popular belief, the Bar Raiser process wasn't handed down by Jeff Bezos. It was engineered in 1999 by software leaders **Rick Dalzell** and **Joel Spiegel**, together with recruiting lead **John Vlastelica**, after they observed a newly hired director's team quietly lowering the company's overall talent bar with each subsequent hire — a direct consequence of "new people hiring new people hiring new people" with no shared standard. Bezos's early interviewing style (SAT scores, brain-teaser questions like "why are manhole covers round?") selected for raw intelligence, not for fit or Amazon's cultural mission — an early lesson that intelligence alone doesn't predict who will "thrive as a missionary, not a mercenary." Once proposed, Bezos endorsed the program and it was quickly made mandatory company-wide.

### 3. The Bar Raiser Mechanism

The design goal was a hiring process that is **scalable, repeatable, teachable, and self-correcting** — explicitly not dependent on a handful of naturally gifted interviewers. Its defining structural feature:

- **An independent veto-holder.** A trained "Bar Raiser" — never the hiring manager or recruiter, and unaffected by the pressure to fill the seat — sits on every interview loop with authority to block a hire outright. In practice the veto is almost never exercised (three times across roughly 4,000 tracked interviews); its real value is deterrent and disciplinary, keeping the bar high by default.
- **A dedicated talent pipeline.** Bar Raisers are hand-selected for interviewing skill and judgment, trained formally, mentored through shadowing, and reviewed by a senior "Bar Raiser Core" — with no extra pay, only reputational status.

### 4. The Eight-Step Hiring Pipeline

| Step | Core discipline it enforces |
|---|---|
| Job Description | Forces specificity before search begins — vague JDs guarantee a mismatched funnel |
| Résumé Review | Recruiter selects against the JD, not general impression |
| Phone Screen | ~45 minutes of structured, Leadership-Principle-mapped questioning; only proceed if already inclined to hire |
| In-House Interview Loop | 5–7 interviewers, each assigned specific Leadership Principles to probe |
| Written Feedback | Mandatory, specific, written before interviewers discuss with each other — blocks confirmation bias at the source |
| Debrief/Hiring Meeting | Bar Raiser-led; votes may change only after everyone reads all feedback, never before |
| Reference Check | Now de-emphasized — rarely changes the outcome |
| Offer Through Onboarding | Hiring manager, not recruiter, personally closes and stays engaged through start date |

Two techniques anchor the loop itself:

- **Behavioral Interviewing (STAR):** Interviewers are each assigned specific Leadership Principles and ask for concrete past examples (Situation, Task, Action, Result) rather than open-ended narrative questions — which reliably produce polished but unverifiable answers.
- **Forced, unhedged votes.** Written feedback ends in one of four choices — strongly hire, hire, not hire, strongly not hire — with no "undecided" option, and interviewers may not see peers' votes until their own is submitted.

### 5. Adaptability Without Diluting the Bar

Amazon explicitly treats the process as a mechanism to be tuned, not a fixed ritual:

- One team added a second phone screen after noticing an above-average in-person no-hire rate, using their own funnel metrics to diagnose where in the pipeline quality was leaking.
- One director discovered that resumes implying a female candidate were being under-selected for phone screens due to unconscious bias — and fixed it by auto-advancing every such résumé to a phone screen, without lowering the bar at any later stage (candidates still had to pass on merit). The result was a measurable, durable improvement in team gender diversity — evidence that structural fixes at the point of bias outperform exhortations to "be less biased."

The chapter is candid that Bar Raiser is not by itself a diversity strategy — achieving that requires deliberate attention to sourcing, JD language, and interview-panel composition — but its bias-suppressing structure (written-first feedback, principle-mapped questions, forced specificity) makes it a useful component of one.

---

### Bottom Line

Amazon's hiring advantage doesn't come from smarter interviewers — it comes from removing the conditions (urgency, unstructured discussion, vague criteria) that let bias substitute for evidence. The transferable lesson: give one person in every hiring decision explicit authority and no stake in filling the seat quickly, require specific written evidence before any group discussion happens, and treat the pipeline's own pass-through metrics as a feedback loop for continuously tuning the process — not just an audit trail.
