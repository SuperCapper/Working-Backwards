# Chapter 4 — Communicating: Narratives and the Six-Pager

### Executive Summary

In 2004, Amazon banned PowerPoint from S-Team meetings and replaced it with silent, in-room reading of six-page written narratives. The change wasn't cosmetic — it was a deliberate mechanism to fix a decision-quality problem: slide decks let presenters gloss over weak reasoning, flatten the relative importance of ideas, and let performance skill (not analytical rigor) determine whether a proposal got approved. A written narrative, by contrast, forces the author to fully articulate cause-and-effect reasoning before anyone in the room reacts to it, and forces the audience to engage with the argument itself rather than the delivery. The transferable principle: when decisions are complex and consequential, the format you require for proposing them is itself a lever on the quality of the decisions you get.

---

### 1. The Problem: PowerPoint as a Decision-Quality Risk

Amazon's weekly four-hour S-Team meeting was expensive — half a day of the company's most senior leaders' time — and increasingly frustrated by slide-based deep dives that were "frustrating, inefficient, and error prone." The diagnosis, crystallized by Jeff Bezos and Colin Bryar reading Edward Tufte's essay *"The Cognitive Style of PowerPoint: Pitching Out Corrupts Within"* on a flight in 2004, was structural rather than a matter of individual presenters' skill:

- **Bullet points degrade exactly the analysis that matters most.** Tufte: *"As analysis becomes more causal, multivariate, comparative, evidence based, and resolution-intense, the more damaging the bullet list becomes."* S-Team decisions were precisely this kind of analysis.
- **Slides force presenters to compress, then verbally "fill back in."** Amazon's own internal guidance held that anything over ~50 words per slide is really a disguised document better read in advance, not presented — yet real decision-relevant content routinely exceeded that, pushing critical information into spoken asides that weren't captured anywhere.
- **Format effects overwhelm content effects.** A charismatic presenter and polished slides could carry a weak idea; a nervous presenter or a plain template could bury a strong one. Decision quality became coupled to public-speaking skill and graphic design — both irrelevant to whether the underlying idea was sound.
- **Live interruption breaks the argument's structure.** Questions arriving mid-deck, sometimes answered on a later slide, forced presenters to double back and derailed the room's collective train of thought.

### 2. The Fix: A Mandated Format Change

On June 9, 2004, Bezos ordered — by company-wide email, the kind of change only he could mandate — that PowerPoint be banned from S-Team meetings in favor of written narratives. The reaction was immediate and almost universally negative, especially from leaders who had to present within the next two weeks and had no established template to work from. Bezos's stated rationale reframed the extra effort as the point, not a cost: *"The reason writing a good 4 page memo is harder than 'writing' a 20 page PowerPoint is because the narrative structure of a good memo forces better thought and better understanding of what's more important than what, and how things are related."*

Early attempts badly missed the target — some narratives ran 30–40 pages; others crammed excessive text into a page via tiny fonts and shrunk margins to dodge the length limit. Amazon converged, through iteration, on the now-standard format: **maximum six pages**, no formatting tricks to cheat the limit, with supporting detail permitted only in appendices that are not required reading during the meeting.

### 3. Why It Works: The Narrative Information Multiplier

The chapter quantifies the format's advantage with what Amazon calls the **Narrative Information Multiplier**: a typical written page carries 3,000–4,000 characters, versus an average of just 440 characters per page across 50 sampled S-Team PowerPoint decks — a **7–9x** difference in information density. Combined with Tufte's observation that people read roughly three times faster than a presenter can talk, a six-page narrative read silently in ~20 minutes transmits dramatically more substantive content than the equivalent live presentation slot, while also permitting **nonlinear, interconnected arguments** that a slide deck's forced linear sequence cannot represent.

### 4. Anatomy of an Effective Six-Pager

There is no single mandated template, but strong narratives share recurring elements:

- **State, support, conclude.** The classic essay structure — build a persuasive, connected argument rather than a disconnected list of assertions the reader must stitch together themselves.
- **Tenets.** An explicit statement of the foundational assumptions the argument rests on, giving readers a clear point to challenge directly instead of relitigating every downstream inference.
- **FAQ section.** Proactively anticipating and answering the objections, misunderstandings, and counterarguments a critical reader would raise — a discipline that itself reveals gaps in the author's own thinking before the meeting happens.
- **Format varies by purpose.** A product proposal and a quarterly business review (Introduction, Tenets, Accomplishments, Misses, Proposals for Next Period, Headcount, P&L, FAQ, Appendices) look different; the constant is density, connectedness, and self-containment — the document must stand on its own without the author present.

### 5. The New Meeting Mechanic

- **Silent reading first, no oral walkthrough.** The first ~20 minutes of the meeting are silent in-room reading; presenters who try to "walk the room through the document" verbally are discouraged from doing so — the whole point is that the reasoning already lives on the page.
- **A dedicated note-taker**, ideally not the presenter, captures the discussion — Colin Bryar describes pausing meetings to ask who is taking notes if no one has volunteered, since the discussion itself becomes part of the narrative's record.
- **Silence equals agreement.** Not objecting during discussion carries the same weight as an explicit endorsement — accountability for the outcome is shared between the writing team and the room that read and didn't push back.

### 6. Feedback as an Adversarial-but-Collaborative Discipline

The chapter's sharpest technique is Bezos's own reading heuristic: **assume every sentence is wrong until you can prove it right** — challenging the claim, not the writer's motive. The worked example: a narrative claiming a 60-day return window (vs. competitors' 30 days) was "customer friendly." A critical reading pushes further — is *making customers wait for the warehouse to receive and verify a return* actually customer-obsessed, or just less bad than the alternative? That line of questioning is credited with producing Amazon's **no-hassle returns policy** (refund issued before the returned item is received, clawed back only from the small fraction who never return the item) — a concrete case of the review process itself generating a better product decision, not merely validating one.

---

### Bottom Line

The six-pager is a mechanism, not a writing-style preference: it forces the *author* to expose and connect their full reasoning before anyone reacts to it, and it forces the *audience* to engage with the argument's substance under a discipline (assume each sentence is wrong until proven otherwise) that a live pitch never imposes. The broader lesson for any organization: if your highest-stakes decisions are being made in a format that structurally rewards delivery over reasoning, changing the format — even at real short-term cost and resistance — is itself a decision-quality intervention, not just a communication-style preference.
